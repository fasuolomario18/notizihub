# Form 990 Revisions: IRS and Treasury Crackdown on Tax-Exempt Organization Fraud

Tax-exempt organizations play a vital role in American society, serving millions through charitable work, religious programs, educational initiatives, and community services. However, ensuring these organizations maintain their tax-exempt status while operating with integrity has become increasingly challenging. In 2026, the Treasury Department and Internal Revenue Service announced substantial revisions to Form 990, the primary tax return filed by tax-exempt organizations. These changes represent a watershed moment for nonprofit accountability and tax compliance, introducing stricter disclosure requirements designed to prevent fraudulent activities and enhance public trust in the nonprofit sector.

## Understanding Form 990 and Its Importance

Form 990 serves as the cornerstone of nonprofit tax reporting in the United States. Tax-exempt organizations—including charitable foundations, educational institutions, religious organizations, and social welfare groups—must file this form annually to maintain their exempt status. The form provides detailed financial information about organizational revenues, expenses, executive compensation, and program activities to the IRS and the general public.

For decades, Form 990 remained relatively unchanged, leading to concerns about outdated reporting standards that couldn't adequately address modern fraudulent schemes. Nonprofit fraud has become increasingly sophisticated, with criminals exploiting gaps in tax reporting requirements to misappropriate funds, overstate charitable work, and engage in self-dealing activities. The Treasury and IRS recognized that existing Form 990 requirements didn't provide sufficient transparency or verification mechanisms to detect these abuses effectively.

The decision to revise Form 990 comes after years of scrutiny from Congress, nonprofit watchdog organizations, and tax professionals who identified critical vulnerabilities in the current system. These revisions represent a comprehensive response to mounting evidence that stronger oversight mechanisms are essential to protecting charitable resources and maintaining public confidence in the tax-exempt sector.

## Key Changes to Form 990 Requirements

The revised Form 990 introduces several groundbreaking changes that will fundamentally alter how tax-exempt organizations report their activities and finances. These modifications enhance transparency, require more detailed disclosures, and implement stronger verification protocols.

**Enhanced Compensation Disclosure**: The revised form now requires organizations to provide detailed breakdowns of executive and key employee compensation packages beyond base salaries. Organizations must now disclose bonuses, deferred compensation arrangements, retirement benefits, and perquisites in greater detail. This increased transparency helps identify inflated compensation schemes that may constitute prohibited private inurement—a critical issue that previous forms often inadequately captured.

**Detailed Program Expense Documentation**: Tax-exempt organizations must now provide substantially more information about how funds are allocated to charitable programs versus administrative and fundraising expenses. The revised form requires itemized breakdowns of significant program expenses, with supporting schedules that detail specific initiatives, beneficiary populations served, and measurable outcomes. This requirement directly addresses concerns that some organizations misclassified fundraising or administrative costs as program expenses.

**Related-Party Transaction Reporting**: The IRS has significantly expanded requirements for disclosing transactions with related parties, including board members, major donors, and affiliated organizations. The revised form requires detailed descriptions of transaction purposes, amounts, and terms, helping investigators identify self-dealing arrangements and potential fraud schemes that exploit organizational assets for personal gain.

**Enhanced Schedule C Reporting**: Schedule C, which details lobbying and political activities, now includes more granular reporting requirements and verification mechanisms. Organizations must provide detailed descriptions of lobbying activities, including specific legislative issues addressed and estimated expenses allocated to each activity.

## Why These Revisions Were Necessary

The urgency behind Form 990 revisions stems from documented cases of nonprofit fraud that previous reporting standards failed to prevent or detect. Several high-profile cases revealed how organizations exploited outdated Form 990 requirements to conceal fraudulent activities.

**Growing Fraud Detection**: IRS audits of tax-exempt organizations have uncovered increasing instances of fraud, diversion of charitable assets, and misrepresentation of program activities. Whistleblowers and nonprofit watchdog organizations documented cases where organizations successfully disguised inflated salaries, excessive perquisites, and personal enrichment schemes as legitimate organizational expenses.

**Public Trust Erosion**: Highly publicized fraud cases damaged public confidence in the nonprofit sector. Major donors became increasingly concerned about whether their charitable contributions actually supported stated missions or instead enriched executives. The revised Form 990 aims to restore confidence by providing donors, regulators, and the public with better information to assess organizational legitimacy and effectiveness.

**Technology Evolution**: The original Form 990 design predated modern financial systems and data analytics capabilities. The revised form leverages contemporary technology to capture data in standardized formats, enabling the IRS to implement sophisticated analytics that flag suspicious patterns and identify organizations requiring closer examination.

**Compliance Burden Reduction**: Paradoxically, while adding disclosure requirements, the revised form actually reduces compliance burdens for legitimate organizations through improved digital filing systems and clearer instructions. Organizations operating with integrity will find the updated form easier to complete accurately, while those engaged in fraudulent activities will face stronger detection mechanisms.

## Implementation Timeline and Compliance Expectations

Tax-exempt organizations must begin using the revised Form 990 for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2026. The IRS implemented a phased approach to provide organizations adequate time for systems adjustments and staff training.

**Phase One** affected large organizations with revenues exceeding $50 million, requiring immediate compliance with all revised requirements. **Phase Two**, beginning with the 2027 tax year, extends requirements to organizations with revenues between $10 million and $50 million. **Phase Three** brings all remaining tax-exempt organizations into full compliance for tax years beginning January 1, 2028.

This phased approach recognizes that smaller organizations may require additional time and resources to implement new internal controls and reporting systems. However, the IRS emphasized that all organizations must begin preparing immediately for compliance, as the transition timeline is fixed and non-negotiable.

**Q: What penalties apply for non-compliance with the revised requirements?**
A: The IRS can impose substantial penalties for incomplete or inaccurate Form 990 filings, including monetary penalties, loss of tax-exempt status, and potential referral for fraud investigation. **Q: How can organizations prepare for Form 990 compliance?**
A: Organizations should immediately consult with tax professionals, update internal accounting systems and controls, implement enhanced documentation procedures, and train staff about new requirements. **Q: Will the revised Form 990 affect donor privacy?**
A: The revised form does not require disclosure of individual donor identities or contribution amounts. However, organizations must disclose more detailed information about how funds are used, potentially affecting how organizations communicate program activities to donors.

**Q: Can organizations request extensions for Form 990 compliance?**
A: The IRS has indicated that the phased implementation timeline is fixed, and extensions for the compliance deadline are extremely limited. Organizations should begin preparation immediately rather than seeking delays.
